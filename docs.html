About this project

This is a proof-of-concept built by the Eric and Wendy Schmidt Center for Data Science & Environment (DSE) at UC Berkeley to explore the data behind the Barred Owl Tool inside the GLEN architecture. It is not affiliated with, endorsed by, or maintained by the tool's authors.

The Barred Owl Tool is a decision-support tool that prioritizes where to concentrate invasive barred owl (Strix varia) population control to protect the northern spotted owl (Strix occidentalis caurina), complementing the USFWS Barred Owl Management Strategy. All of the science, modeling (via the Zonation algorithm), and the original interactive tool are the work of its authors — full credit and authority rest with them.

Use the official tool. For authoritative results, methods, and guidance, please visit the original application and its documentation:

  • barredowltool.russell.wisc.edu — the official Barred Owl Tool (Background, Methods, How-to guide)
  • USFWS Barred Owl Management — the management strategy this tool supports

Coproduced by the University of Wisconsin–Madison Peery Wildlife Ecology + Conservation Lab and the US Fish & Wildlife Service, with support from NASA's Ecological Conservation program.

Understanding the tool

The conservation problem

The northern spotted owl (NSO), Strix occidentalis caurina, is a threatened old-forest raptor of the Pacific Northwest. The barred owl, Strix varia — a larger, more aggressive relative from eastern North America — has expanded into the spotted owl's range and now outcompetes it for territory and food. This invasion is one of the leading drivers of the spotted owl's continued decline. The USFWS Barred Owl Management Strategy calls for targeted removal of barred owls where it will most benefit spotted owls. But the range is vast and resources are limited, so the core question is where to act — a spatial prioritization problem.

What a "priority run" is

The tool answers that question with Zonation, a systematic conservation-planning algorithm. Given a set of input layers — where spotted owls are, where barred owls are, habitat quality, fire risk, accessibility, and so on — Zonation ranks every ~5 km² hex cell by how valuable it is for barred owl management, producing a priority score from 0 to 1 (higher = higher priority). One such ranking is a priority run. The score is relative: it ranks cells against each other within a chosen planning area, rather than measuring an absolute amount — so scores from different planning areas aren't directly comparable.

What the scenarios are

There is no single "correct" prioritization — it depends on management goals and assumptions. The tool therefore produces 2,754 scenarios, combining four choices:

  • Where you're planning (management unit): the whole spotted owl range, one of 10 physiographic provinces, or one of 40 general management areas. Cells are re-ranked within each unit, so a cell's range-wide score differs from its score inside a province or management area.
  • What you're protecting (threat mode): current spotted owl populations, projected future populations, or nest/roost habitat.
  • Which factor you emphasize (tuning parameter): the weight on spotted owls, or the penalty/incentive for fire risk, fire refugia, federal reserve lands, distance to roads, or distance to trails.
  • How strongly (weight level): low, medium, or high emphasis on that factor.

Comparing scenarios is the point: cells that rank high across many scenarios are robust priorities, while cells that rank high only under one set of assumptions are more sensitive to how the problem is framed.

How it works

This app is powered by GLEN, DSE's open-source framework for AI-assisted geospatial exploration. Open datasets stream straight to your browser from cloud-native formats — no tile server in between — and an AI assistant answers questions by writing live queries against the same data. Technical details are in the GLEN documentation.

What you can explore here

On the map — the model inputs

Each map layer is one of the ingredients the prioritization draws on, shown on the native ~5 km² hex grid. These are the same in every scenario — only the priority ranking changes. From coarse to fine:

  • NSO carrying capacity (0–20) — how many spotted owl territories a cell could support.
  • NSO pair occupancy and nest/roost habitat (0–1) — where spotted owls are, and where suitable breeding habitat is.
  • Barred owl occupancy (0–1) — where the invading barred owl is established.
  • Large-fire suitability vs. fire refugia (0–100) — wildfire risk against climate-buffered refuges.
  • Federal reserve allocation — federal conservation land, which can raise or lower priority depending on the scenario.

Through the assistant — the priority scores

The 2,754 priority runs are far too many to list as map toggles, so they live in a queryable table that the assistant reads on demand. Ask it to map or rank any scenario, or to compare across scenarios. It can also drive the map and answer quantitative questions about the inputs. Try:

  • "Where is northern spotted owl carrying capacity highest?"
  • "Show the range-wide current-threat scenario at high NSO weight."
  • "Compare priority scores between the current-threat and future-threat scenarios."
  • "Which physiographic province has the most high-priority area?"

Reading the scores: higher = higher management priority, and scores are relative within each management unit — so don't compare a province's raw scores against the range-wide run as if they were on one scale.

Data & sources

Data mirror: STAC collection (public-barred-owl) — processed by the Boettiger Lab from the Barred Owl Tool. Underlying inputs include NSO pair occupancy (Lesmeister et al.), spotted-owl nest/roost habitat (Davis et al. 2022), barred-owl landscape use (Jenkins et al. 2025, J. Biogeography), USGS GAP species, large-fire suitability (Davis et al. 2017), OSU Eco-Vis fire refugia, and NWFP federal reserve allocations. See the official tool's Methods page for full provenance.
